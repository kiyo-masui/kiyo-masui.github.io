Research

I am interested in the use of use of the 21 cm line for cosmology. Neutral atomic hydrogen is the most abundant form of matter in the universe aside from the elusive dark matter. Hydrogen has a hyperfine energy transition between aligned proton and electron spins and anti-aligned spins. Transitions between these levels can either emit or absorb a photon with 21 cm wavelength, which can then be detected by radio telescopes. In addition the expansion of the universe stretches photons that have spend more time in transit and thus come from further away. We can thus determine from which epoch (redshift) the observed radiation originated.

This radiation can be used to map the distribution of neutral hydrogen in the universe. One usually assumes that on large scales (say larger than a thousand galaxies) the hydrogen just traces the dark matter, so we can essentially map the distribution of dark matter. The statistics of the distribution of dark matter is one of the key observables in cosmology and can tell us all sorts of things about the universe, from the initial conditions to its late time expansion. Aside from studying dark matter, 21 cm radiation can also be used to study a phase transition in the hydrogen that occurred 1 billion years after the big bang.

The distribution of dark matter is also often studied by surveys that count galaxies (which are mostly made of dark matter) that are usually detected in the optical bands. 21 cm radiation has several advantages, the most important being that the universe is transparent to it at all epochs. Galaxy surveys are difficult in certain redshift ranges. There is also a epoch before the formation of the first galaxies, the so called dark ages, that is only accessible via 21 cm radiation. This has great statistical potential since most of the observable volume of the universe is in this epoch.

The use of 21 cm radiation for cosmology is a fairly new idea and no one has ever actually made a blind detection of 21 cm radiation coming from any cosmologically significant distance. I am working toward making these first detections and performing early science. I also like to think about the types of measurements that might eventually be possible given the extraordinary potential of this technique.

Current Projects

Here I'll give a quick summary of things I'm currently thinking about. This section is a bit more technical than above.

Hydrogen surveys using existing telescopes

With Jeff Peterson's group, Tzu-Ching Chang, and my adviser Ue-Li, I am part of a team that is performing 21 cm surveys using existing telescopes such as the Green Bank Telescope. We are targeting redshifts around z=0.8 and are trying to make an initial detection in auto correlation. The group has already detected the hydrogen by cross correlating with an optical galaxy survey. This was published here, with popular science articles here and here. While there is much that can be learnt along the way, the ultimate goal is to measure the baryon acoustic oscillations and constrain the accelerated expansion of the universe.

Foreground subtraction

The use of 21 cm radiation for cosmology is plagued by foregrounds. These is mostly synchrotron radiation from either within our own galaxy or from other galaxies, and subtracting them out is tricky business because they are so much brighter than the signal. We think that it is possible to subtract them out because the foregrounds are spectrally smooth, while at each redshift hydrogen emits at only the one frequency. When stacking all the redshifts together, the spectrum of an angular pixel has spectral structure corresponding to the hydrogen density variations along the line of sight. Fourier modes corresponding to small scales along the line of sight should be uncontaminated by foregrounds. This can still be complicated because experimental effects can cause the different Fourier modes to mix in a complicated way. As part of my involvement in hydrogen surveys, I am always thinking about the best way to remove foregrounds to get at the 21 cm signal.

Gravity wave fossils

If 21 cm radiation opens up the dark ages for observation, it will allow us to map the dark matter over huge volumes (hundreds of cubic gigaparsecs) and down to very small scales (tens of kiloparsecs). The number of independent observable Fourier modes would be immense and such good statistics would allow for the detection of many subtle effects. One of these effects is a signature of primordial gravity waves described in this paper. While it turns out you would need a telescope 100 times the size of the proposed Square Kilometer Array to have a chance of seeing anything, the effect may nevertheless one day be observed.
